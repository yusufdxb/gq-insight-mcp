[00:31] Interviewer: How did pricing factor into your decision?
[00:44] P-2210: The headline price looked great. Twelve dollars a seat, fine. What I didn't catch was that "seat" means anyone who submits an expense, not just admins. So overnight our seat count went from four to thirty-one.
[02:05] Interviewer: Were you surprised by the bill?
[02:11] P-2210: Blindsided. The first invoice was almost four times what I'd modeled. Nobody on sales walked me through how seats were counted, and the pricing page was vague about it.
[04:18] P-2210: I get that submitters cost money, but charge me a cheaper submitter tier then. An employee who files one receipt a month should not cost the same as my controller who lives in the tool.
[06:40] Interviewer: Did you consider leaving over it?
[06:48] P-2210: I escalated. They gave us a one-time credit, which helped, but it left a bad taste. Pricing surprises erode trust faster than a missing feature does.
[08:55] P-2210: If the pricing page had one honest sentence — "you pay per person who submits" — I'd have planned for it and we'd be fine. It's the surprise that stings, not the number.
